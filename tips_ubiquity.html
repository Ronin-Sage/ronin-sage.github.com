:: Ubiquity -- Little-known, Much-awesome ::

The best thing(and some would argue the only particularly useful) thing about Firefox is its diverse library of add-ons. There's pretty much a plugin for every purpose out there, and some, such as AdBlock Plus, DownloadThemAll, and a few others are known to be particularly prominent in the FF community to the point that they're considered must-haves.

One add-on that you may or may not have heard of, but definitely should look into is Ubiquity. Summarily, it's OS X's Quicksilver for Firefox -- a "run dialog" of sorts that lets you do pretty much...well, anything. Print or save a page, open a new tab, run a search using whatever search engine, define or translate words, anything.

There are two notable things about Ubiquity.

One is that its extremely customizable: every available command exists in the form of a block of code(Javascript, mind you), meaning creating new commands, their parameters, descriptions, and even effects is within your reach(given that you have enough Javascript-coding experience). Moreover, styles/themes are actually just CSS files.

The other great thing about Ubiquity is that executing commands is done in a uniquely "human" way. For instance, e-mailing a webpage to someone is done by entering "email this to bob".

Mind you, it's still experimental, but even-so, I can honestly say that this one add-on makes Firefox a great deal more enjoyable to use and more effective as a web browser.
