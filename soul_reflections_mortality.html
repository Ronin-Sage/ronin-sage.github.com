:: "On Mortality" ::

Death.

The very word summons notions of some terrible cosmic truth, images of an interplanar conference with a ghastly figure of another world, some harbinger of inescapable doom. But why?

Death, by its very nature, is the sibling facet of life, both child elements of existence. It's both natural and inevtiable that all living things must eventually die. Still, this logic serves as no comfort when we are faced with the cold reality of our own measured mortality.

It's fear, more than anything else, that steeps the concept of death in what can only be described as elemental terror. It is our nature, after all, to live, and in the greatest example of irony, we condemn the most natural thing of all as being unnatural in manner.

Perhaps it is not the finality that death brings, but the utter lack of control we have over it that drives us to fear and despise it like an evil thing. Almost universally across differnet cultuers, death is personified as a sinister wraith that lurks utterly beyond our comprehension or jurisdiction.

This is the way we perceive it, but should it not be otherwise?

Perhaps the end of life should be seen instead as a mystery, an opportunity to explore the unknown. After all, none can know what lies beyond. Only as individuals can we truly reconcile with our brief existence in this plane, making sense out of what a cursory glance would suggest is a meaningless existence.
